In the rapidly advancing field of terahertz (THz) and submillimeter-wave technology, precision measurement and system integration demand components that operate flawlessly across a vast frequency spectrum. One of the most critical passive components is the waveguide termination (or load). Its primary function is to absorb incident electromagnetic energy with minimal reflection, thereby providing a matched, low-VSWR port essential for protecting sources, improving measurement accuracy, and ensuring stable system performance.

We are proud to announce that we offer a complete family of high-performance terahertz terminations and submillimeter-wave loads for all standard WR waveguide sizes. Our portfolio includes not only the commonly used WR-10, WR-8, WR-6.5, WR-5.1, WR-4.3, WR-3.4, and WR-2.8, but also the full range of smaller, harder-to-fabricate waveguides: WR-2.2, WR-1.5, WR-1.2, WR-1.0, and WR-3. Every termination is precision-machined with standard UG-style or IEEE 1785.2 flanges, ensuring seamless integration with existing systems. Whether your application is in atmospheric sensing, security imaging, or next-generation communications, you can rely on our terminations for low return loss, stable absorption, and repeatable performance across the entire submillimeter-wave spectrum—from 75 GHz up to 1.1 THz and beyond. Choose us for one-stop, full-waveguide coverage.

The key to proper operation lies in matching the termination’s waveguide interface to the operating frequency band. Rectangular waveguides, designated by the standard “WR” (Waveguide Rectangular) number, have physical dimensions that directly determine their cutoff frequency and usable range. Consequently, each frequency band requires a specific waveguide size. For example, WR-10 covers 75–110 GHz (W-band), WR-6.5 spans 110–170 GHz (D-band), WR-4.3 operates from 170–260 GHz, and WR-2.8 covers 260–400 GHz. As frequencies climb into the submillimeter-wave region, even smaller waveguides are used: WR-2.2 (330–500 GHz), WR-1.5 (500–750 GHz), and WR-1.0 (750–1100 GHz), which extends into the 1 THz range.
